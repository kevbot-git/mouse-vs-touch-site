Design

This test will be specifically designed to compare touch and mouse input devices, and to hopefully discover which device is more effective at pointing to objects on a laptop screen. To perform this usability test, we will create a small web application for participants to play which aims to both to be enjoyable for participants, and to give us meaningful data to analyse, that is consistent enough to make a call on how results compare with our hypothesis. The test will work by successively displaying circles on the screen for the participant to click them or touch (depending on the input device), as promptly as possible. The size and position of the circles that the participant click will be completely randomized; with the size of the circles ranging from 20 to 120 pixels in diameter and the position being randomly placed on the screen (the circles will be bound to the screen dimensions).

Before starting the test, we will assign participants either mouse or touch input to start with (we would alternate starts in effort to counter learning bias), and after explaining how to play, ask them to start by pressing a button in the top-left corner of the screen. The test will start, and they will point at 20 targets, change to the remaining input device and repeat (again, with new sizes and positions). We will explain the test instructions to participants carefully in effort to prevent them from being uncertain about what to do; as misunderstanding the instructions could lead to corrupt data and extreme outliers etc. Informing the participant as opposed to explaining the purpose or advising any particular action that the participant should take will be important due to the trivial and fast-paced nature of the test (we estimate it to take less than a minute for each participant) (Lauesen, S., 2005, p. 443).

We will randomize the radius and position of the circles that the participant is pressing in order to eliminate the possibility that the participant will recall the positions of circles on their second time through. This is important because the dependent variable of our experiment is the timeframe in which the participant can press objects, and “more practice results in less time needed to complete tasks”. (Sauro, J.,, 2013) We will also try to mitigate this issue by setting the target count to 20 objects per input method, so that the probability that the participant would get objects of similar size and location would be lower, which might skew the results.

Our participants will be selected at random from the area surrounding the room in which we will perform our experiment (most likely in WT501), using hallway testing. In researching the ideal number of participants for our study we learnt that it is both cost and time effective—while also providing near synonymous results to a large group of participants—to use a small set of participants. Nielson (2000) argues that testing a single participant can give you 30% of the information you were looking for in your experiment. Gaining another participant will give you some of the same insight as before, but also provide you with new and unique data, and as the number increases, the things able to be learnt from the test quickly deplete. What we took away from this was that by keeping to a low number of participants (11) we were almost guaranteed to get the same findings from our experiment, while saving more time locating and convincing people to participate in our study.

Hypothesis

We hypothesize that the relationship between targets’ sizes and displacement from the cursor’s original position, and the time users take to point to it using touch will be closer to linear in nature in comparison to that in those using mouse input. We also think, however, that due to users’ fingers presenting parallax type errors (we expect this will be more noticeable with smaller targets), mouse pointing might become more accurate below a particular target size and therefore, potentially, make pointing more efficient than with touch the smaller the targets are.

Execution

  • clear_allList of Steps
    1. Find someone who is willing to participate in the experiment (we will be implementing hallway testing)
    2. Ask the participant to read the experiment’s Information Sheet
    3. Ask them to sign a Consent Form if they agree to its terms
    4. Ask them to sit at the desk in front of the test device if they have not already done so, in a way that is comfortable to them; they can adjust their seat to do so
    5. Explain to the them that they are asked to start the test by clicking the ‘Start’ button; from which point they will be successively presented with circular targets they must click as fast as they can
    6. Explain to them that we will assign them either mouse or touch input as their first test (we will alternate the start device between participants), afterwhich they will use the remaining input device for the second and final test
    7. Ask them to start once they have understood the instructions and are ready
    8. Once they reach the end (after 20 targets have been clicked), ask them to prepare to repeat using the remaining input device
    9. Prepare the test and ask them to start
    10. Once they are finished, thank them for their participation
